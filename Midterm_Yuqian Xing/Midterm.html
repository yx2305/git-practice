Task 1: See how communication design can make a difference.

Compare the following two messages, and then answer the questions below:

The situation: Every algebra teacher in recorded history has had to deal with two student questions:"Why do I need to know this? When will I ever use this?" There two attempts to answer these questions.

    Statement 1

  • Algebra provides methods for moving from the specific to the general. It involves discovering the patterns among items in a set and developing the language needed to think about and communicate it to others.
  • Algebra provides precedures for manipulating symbols to allow for understanding the world around us.
  • Algebra provides a vehicle for understanding our world through mathematical models.
  • Algebra is the science of variables. It enables us to deal with large bodies of data by identifying variables and by imposing or finding structures within the data.
  • Algebra is the basic set of ideas and techniques for describing and reasoning about relations between variable quantities.

    Statement 2

  • My grade 9 students ask me when they are ever going to need this.I say,"Never. You will never use this."
  • I then go on to remind them that people don't lift weights so that they will be prepared should, one day,someone knock them over on the street and lay a barbell across their chests. You lift weights so that you can knock over a defensive lineman, or carry your groceries or lift your grandchildren without being sore the next day. You do math exercises so that you can improve your ability to think logically, so that you can be a better lawyer, doctor, architect, prison warden or parent.
  • MATH IS MENTAL WEIGHT TRAINING. It is a means to an end, not an end in itself.

Question 2: Why do you think the statement you choose is better than the other?

Although the first statement seems quite logical, any restless student probably would not jump on the algebra bandwagon after being told this message. As reasons for studying algebra, it won't work because of its abstractness and complexity. In contrast, the second message is a great response. The use of analogy and detailed discription makes the message simple and concrete: it's not that the future you're going to have a daily need to find the slope of a line; it's that your are making your brain more muscular. Also, the second message taps into your desire to succeed because it conveys the idea that learning algebra makes you realize more of your potential.

In short, messages that are 1) simple rather than complex, 2) concrete rather than abstract, and 3) appealing to people's emotion rather than their logic are more likely to exert influence on people's thought and behavior. Please be sure to memorize these three techniques. You will need to apply them in later tasks.
